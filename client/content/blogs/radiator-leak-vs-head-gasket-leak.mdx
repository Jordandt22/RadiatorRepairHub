Both problems involve coolant loss and overheating, but a radiator leak and a head gasket leak are very different repairs. Knowing which you are dealing with helps you avoid driving into a much bigger bill. Start with [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak) if you are trying to confirm coolant loss first.

## What is a radiator leak?

A **radiator leak** means coolant is escaping **outside** the engine — usually from the radiator itself, a hose, a clamp, the water pump, or the radiator cap. You may see:

- Puddles or wet spots under the front of the car
- A sweet smell near the engine bay
- Low coolant in the reservoir without obvious exhaust issues

Repairs often involve replacing a hose, sealing a radiator, or installing a new radiator. Many fixes are straightforward when caught early. ## What is a head gasket leak?

The **head gasket** seals the engine block and cylinder head. When it fails, coolant can leak **internally** — into combustion chambers or oil passages — rather than dripping onto the ground.

Common signs include:

- **White smoke from the exhaust** (especially on startup) that smells sweet
- **Milky or frothy oil** on the dipstick or oil cap
- **Bubbles in the radiator** with the engine running (needs professional diagnosis)
- Overheating with **no visible external leak**
- Coolant disappearing quickly with no puddle

Head gasket repairs are significantly more expensive and labor-intensive than most radiator fixes. Do not keep driving if you suspect this problem — see [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak) and treat overheating the same way regardless of cause.

## Quick comparison

- **Radiator leak** — External drip, wet hoses or radiator, often visible leak point, usually lower repair cost
- **Head gasket leak** — White exhaust smoke, contaminated oil, overheating without a puddle, major engine repair

## How a shop tells the difference

A qualified technician may use:

1. **Pressure test** — Checks the cooling system for external leaks
2. **Combustion leak test** — Detects exhaust gases in the coolant (head gasket indicator)
3. **Oil and coolant inspection** — Looks for cross-contamination
4. **Compression test** — Evaluates cylinder sealing

This is not a DIY diagnosis. A [radiator repair specialist](/category/radiator-repair-service) or experienced [auto repair shop](/category/auto-repair-shop) can pinpoint the issue before recommending the right fix.

## What to do if you are unsure

1. **Stop driving** if the temperature gauge is high or you see steam.
2. **Check for external leaks** only when the engine is cold.
3. **Note exhaust color and oil condition** — write down what you see for the technician.
4. **Get a professional opinion** before assuming it is a simple hose or radiator problem.
