Finding a puddle or stain under your car can be alarming — especially when the fluid is sweet-smelling and brightly colored. That is usually **coolant (antifreeze)**, and it means your cooling system is losing fluid somewhere. Acting quickly helps you avoid overheating and expensive engine damage.

For a broader look at leak warning signs, see [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak).

## How to tell if it is coolant

Coolant is often **green, orange, pink, blue, or yellow** depending on the formula. It typically has a **sweet smell** and a slightly sticky texture — unlike oil (dark and slick) or water from the A/C (clear, odorless, usually under the passenger side).

If you are unsure, place a white paper towel under the leak and note the color and smell when the engine is cool.

## Common causes of coolant leaks under the car

### Radiator leak

The radiator itself can develop cracks in plastic tanks, corroded seams, or damage from road debris. Leaks often appear **under the front center** of the vehicle.

### Hose failure

Rubber hoses harden and crack with age. A blown hose can dump coolant quickly. Clamps can also loosen or rust, causing seepage at connection points.

### Water pump weep hole

Many water pumps have a small drain hole that leaks when the internal seal fails. You may see drips near the front of the engine, often behind the drive belt area.

### Radiator cap or overflow tank

A worn cap may not hold pressure, forcing coolant into the overflow reservoir or out through the cap seal. Cracked overflow tanks also leak onto the ground.

### Heater core (inside the cabin)

If you smell coolant **inside** the car or see wet carpet on the passenger floor, the heater core may be leaking — even if you also see fluid outside.

### Head gasket (internal leak)

Not all gasket failures drip onto the pavement. White exhaust smoke or milky oil without an obvious puddle can still mean coolant loss. Read [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak) to understand the difference.

## What to do when you find a leak

1. **Do not drive if the temperature gauge is high** or you lost a large amount of fluid quickly.
2. **Let the engine cool completely** before opening the radiator cap or reservoir.
3. **Check the coolant level** in the overflow tank — only top off with the correct type if you are low and need to reach a shop.
4. **Look for the source** — hoses, radiator, water pump area, and cap.
5. **Schedule a diagnosis** at a [radiator repair specialist](/category/radiator-repair-service) or [radiator shop](/category/radiator-shop).

Before driving even a short distance, read [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak).

## What not to do

- **Do not ignore a small drip** — it often gets worse
- **Do not mix coolant types** without knowing what is already in the system
- **Do not rely on stop leak long-term** — see [Is Radiator Stop Leak Safe?](/blogs/is-radiator-stop-leak-safe)
- **Do not open a hot radiator cap** — pressurized coolant can cause severe burns

## How a shop finds the source

Technicians typically pressure-test the cooling system, inspect hoses and clamps, check the water pump and radiator, and look for head gasket indicators if no external leak appears. A proper diagnosis saves money compared to guessing and replacing the wrong part.
