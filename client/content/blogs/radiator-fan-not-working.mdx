Your radiator cooling fan keeps air moving through the radiator when the car is at idle or stuck in traffic — speeds when there is little natural airflow. When the fan fails, the engine often overheats in stop-and-go driving even if highway trips seemed fine.

If the temperature gauge is already climbing, read [Why Is My Car Overheating?](/blogs/why-is-my-car-overheating) and stop safely. Do not open a hot radiator cap.

## How the cooling fan works

Most modern vehicles use an electric fan (or dual fans) controlled by a temperature sensor, the engine computer, and a relay or fan control module. Older vehicles may use a mechanical clutch fan driven by a belt.

When coolant temperature rises — or when the A/C is on — the fan should turn on to pull or push air through the radiator fins.

## Signs your radiator fan is not working

- Overheating in traffic or at idle, but cooler temps at highway speeds
- Fan never spins when the engine is hot (with A/C on or temperature rising)
- A/C blows warm in traffic because high-side pressures climb without fan airflow
- Temperature warning light in city driving
- No sound of the fan kicking on when you expect it to

These symptoms can overlap with radiator or thermostat problems. Compare patterns in [Thermostat vs Radiator: What's Causing Overheating?](/blogs/thermostat-vs-radiator-whats-causing-overheating) and [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair).

## Common causes

1. **Failed fan motor** — Bearings seize or the motor burns out
2. **Blown fuse or bad relay** — Power never reaches the fan
3. **Faulty coolant temperature sensor** — The computer never commands the fan on
4. **Wiring or connector issues** — Corrosion, broken wires, or damaged plugs
5. **Failed fan control module** — Common on some vehicle designs
6. **Mechanical clutch fan failure** — On older belt-driven systems, the clutch can stick or freewheel

Debris stuck in the fan shroud can also lock the blades — do not reach into a fan that may start unexpectedly.

## What not to do

- Do not keep driving in traffic with a rising gauge hoping it "levels out"
- Do not assume a radiator flush will fix a dead fan — circulation through the core is not the same as airflow across the core
- Do not rely on stop leak products for overheating — see [Is Radiator Stop Leak Safe?](/blogs/is-radiator-stop-leak-safe)

A bad fan can cause repeated overheating that damages the radiator and engine. Learn more in [Can a Bad Radiator Damage Your Engine?](/blogs/can-a-bad-radiator-damage-your-engine).

## How shops diagnose fan problems

A technician will typically:

1. Verify fan operation with a scan tool or by commanding the fan on
2. Check fuses, relays, and voltage at the fan connector
3. Inspect wiring and the temperature sensor signal
4. Confirm the radiator is not clogged and the thermostat is opening
5. Test A/C-related fan requests on systems where A/C demands high fan speed

Many [auto repair shops](/category/auto-repair-shop) and [radiator repair](/category/radiator-repair-service) specialists handle electric fan diagnosis. Mechanical clutch fans are a common job for shops that work on older trucks and SUVs.

## Typical repair options, timing, and cost

Ballpark U.S. ranges once the diagnosis is clear (always get a written quote for your vehicle):

- **Fuse or relay:** Often under an hour; roughly **$20–$150**
- **Temperature sensor:** Moderate cost; diagnose first so you do not replace the fan unnecessarily
- **Fan motor / fan assembly:** About **1–3 hours** and roughly **$250–$600+** parts and labor; dual-fan or hard-to-access setups can run higher
- **Clutch fan:** Replacement of the clutch or fan assembly; pricing varies widely on older trucks and SUVs

Ask how long the job will take — see [How Long Does Radiator Repair Take?](/blogs/how-long-does-radiator-repair-take) — and request a written estimate. Related cooling repair prices are in our [Radiator Repair Cost Guide](/blogs/radiator-repair-cost-guide).

## After the repair

Confirm:

- The fan runs when commanded or when temperature / A/C conditions require it
- The temperature gauge stays normal in traffic
- There are no coolant leaks introduced during the job
- Coolant level is correct after any system opening

If overheating continues after a new fan, the shop should recheck the radiator, thermostat, and for internal leaks — see [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak).
