When the temperature gauge climbs, two common culprits are a stuck thermostat and a failing radiator. They can produce similar symptoms — but the diagnosis and repair are different. Understanding the difference helps you describe the problem accurately and avoid replacing the wrong part.

For a full list of overheating causes, start with [Why Is My Car Overheating?](/blogs/why-is-my-car-overheating). If you also see coolant under the car, see [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak).

## Quick safety reminder

If the gauge is in the red or you see steam:

1. Pull over safely and turn off the engine
2. Do not open the radiator cap while hot
3. Wait for a full cool-down before checking coolant
4. Tow the vehicle if symptoms return — do not keep driving hot

Ignored overheating can lead to serious engine damage — read [Can a Bad Radiator Damage Your Engine?](/blogs/can-a-bad-radiator-damage-your-engine).

## What the thermostat does

The thermostat is a valve that controls coolant flow between the engine and the radiator. When the engine is cold, it stays closed so the engine can warm up. When the engine reaches operating temperature, it opens so hot coolant can reach the radiator and cool down.

### Signs of a stuck thermostat (often stuck closed)

- Overheating soon after starting to drive, sometimes within minutes
- Upper radiator hose stays cool while the engine is very hot
- Temperature spikes quickly, then may drop if the thermostat finally opens
- Heater may blow little or no heat if flow is blocked

A stuck-open thermostat usually causes the opposite problem: the engine takes forever to warm up, not overheating.

## What the radiator does

The radiator transfers heat from the coolant into the air flowing through its fins. If it is clogged, leaking, or damaged, it cannot reject heat even when the thermostat and water pump are working.

### Signs of radiator problems

- Overheating after longer drives or in traffic (heat builds as the radiator fails to keep up)
- Coolant puddles or crusty residue at the radiator or hose connections
- Consistently low coolant levels
- Visible fin damage, soft or cracked plastic end tanks
- Overheating that improves only slightly at highway speeds, or not at all

Compare these with [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair). A clogged core may need a [flush](/blogs/radiator-flush-what-to-expect-and-cost) — or replacement if flow does not return.

## Thermostat vs radiator at a glance

- **Fast overheating after startup** — Often thermostat (stuck closed)
- **Upper radiator hose cold, engine hot** — Strong thermostat clue
- **Puddles / low coolant / crusty leaks** — More often radiator, hose, or pump
- **Overheating mainly in traffic at idle** — Could also be the cooling fan — see [Radiator Fan Not Working](/blogs/radiator-fan-not-working)
- **Slow heat buildup on long highway trips** — Often radiator flow or clogging

These patterns are guides, not a DIY diagnosis. Other parts (water pump, head gasket, radiator cap) can mimic either issue.

## Other parts that look like both

### Radiator cap

A weak cap cannot hold pressure, which lowers the boiling point and can cause intermittent overheating or overflow. See [Radiator Cap Symptoms & Replacement](/blogs/radiator-cap-symptoms-and-replacement).

### Water pump

If the pump impeller is worn or the pump is leaking, coolant may not circulate — similar to a stuck thermostat but with different hose and pulley clues.

### Head gasket

Internal coolant loss can overheat the engine without a puddle. See [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak).

## How a shop tells them apart

A technician will typically:

1. Pressure-test for external leaks at the radiator and hoses
2. Check thermostat opening temperature (on-vehicle or on a bench)
3. Inspect radiator flow, tanks, and fins
4. Verify cooling fan operation at idle
5. Test for combustion gases in the coolant if a head gasket is suspected

Do not guess and replace the radiator first "just in case." Accurate diagnosis often saves money. Typical thermostat and radiator costs are covered in our [Radiator Repair Cost Guide](/blogs/radiator-repair-cost-guide).

## Repair vs replace

- **Failed thermostat** — Usually replace the thermostat (and often the gasket or housing)
- **Leaking or severely clogged radiator** — Repair is limited; replacement is common — see [Radiator Repair vs Replacement](/blogs/radiator-repair-vs-replacement-which-is-better)
- **Both failed or neglected together** — A full cooling system inspection protects the engine
