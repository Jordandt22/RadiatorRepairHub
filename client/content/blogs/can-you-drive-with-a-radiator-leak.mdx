A radiator leak is not something to ignore, but the answer depends on how bad the leak is and whether your engine is already overheating. In most cases, you should limit driving and get the cooling system checked as soon as possible. Not sure if you have a leak yet? Start with [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak).

## When you should not drive

**Stop driving and pull over safely if you notice any of these:**

- Temperature gauge in the red or warning light on
- Steam coming from under the hood
- Strong sweet smell of coolant inside or outside the car
- Coolant pouring out quickly (large puddle forming while parked or driving)
- Engine running rough, losing power, or making unusual noises

Continuing to drive in these conditions can cause **warped heads, blown head gaskets, or complete engine failure** — repairs that cost far more than fixing a leak early.

## When a short drive might be okay

A **very slow seep** — a small damp spot, not a steady drip — might allow a careful trip to a nearby shop, but only if:

1. The temperature gauge stays in the normal range
2. You have enough coolant in the reservoir (check when the engine is cold)
3. The drive is short and you can monitor the gauge the entire time
4. You are not towing, climbing hills, or sitting in heavy stop-and-go traffic

Even then, treat it as a temporary exception. The leak will usually get worse.

## Risks of driving with a leak

- **Low coolant** — Less heat is carried away from the engine
- **Air in the system** — Creates hot spots and uneven cooling
- **Overheating** — Can damage seals, gaskets, and cylinder heads
- **Breakdown** — You may stall in traffic or far from help

Coolant also helps prevent corrosion inside the engine. Running low for too long can damage water pumps, hoses, and other components even if you do not overheat right away.

## What to do before you drive anywhere

1. **Let the engine cool completely** before opening the radiator cap or reservoir.
2. **Check coolant level** in the overflow tank. Only top off when cold, and use the correct coolant type for your vehicle when possible. Plain water is an emergency stopgap only — it dilutes corrosion protection and freeze protection until you can refill properly.
3. **Look for the source** — hose, radiator seam, water pump weep hole, or cap — see also [Radiator Cap Symptoms & Replacement](/blogs/radiator-cap-symptoms-and-replacement).
4. **Plan the shortest route** to a [radiator repair shop](/category/radiator-repair-service) or [radiator shop](/category/radiator-shop) and avoid hard acceleration.
5. **Bring coolant** (and a tow number) in case the gauge climbs — if it does, stop and get a tow rather than keep topping off on the roadside.

If you are unsure, **call a tow truck**. It is cheaper than replacing an engine. Avoid temporary chemical sealants unless a technician recommends one for a specific limp-home situation — read [Is Radiator Stop Leak Safe?](/blogs/is-radiator-stop-leak-safe).

## How radiator shops can help

A specialist can pressure-test the cooling system, locate the exact leak, and tell you whether you need a hose, radiator, cap, or a larger repair. Catching a small leak early often means a simple fix instead of an emergency on the side of the road. Many drivers also find help through local [auto repair shops](/category/auto-repair-shop) that offer cooling system service.
