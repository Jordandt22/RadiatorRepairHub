A radiator flush removes old coolant, rust, and debris from your cooling system and replaces it with fresh fluid. It is routine maintenance for many vehicles — but it is not always the right fix if you already have a leak or overheating problem. If your car is running hot, start with [Why Is My Car Overheating?](/blogs/why-is-my-car-overheating) before scheduling a flush alone.

## What is a radiator flush?

A **radiator flush** (also called a coolant flush or cooling system service) typically includes:

1. Draining old coolant from the radiator and engine
2. Running a cleaning solution or distilled water through the system to remove sediment
3. Refilling with new coolant at the correct mix for your vehicle
4. Bleeding air from the system and checking for leaks

The goal is clean fluid, proper heat transfer, and corrosion protection — not to fix a failed radiator or blown gasket on its own.

## When do you need a flush?

Consider a flush when:

- Your owner manual recommends it (often every 30,000–50,000 miles on older formulas; some extended-life coolants last much longer — always follow the OEM interval)
- Coolant looks rusty, cloudy, or contaminated
- You are replacing a major cooling component (radiator, water pump, thermostat)
- A shop finds sludge during a routine inspection

A flush alone will **not** fix an external leak. See [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak) if you are losing fluid between services.

## What to expect at the shop

Most flushes take **1–2 hours**. The technician should:

- Confirm the correct coolant type for your make and model
- Inspect hoses, clamps, and the radiator cap while the system is open
- Check for leaks after refilling and running the engine
- Dispose of old coolant properly (it is hazardous waste)

Ask whether the quote includes the full system flush or only a drain-and-fill — they are not always the same service.

## How much does a radiator flush cost?

Costs vary by location, vehicle, and coolant type. Typical ranges:

- **Basic drain and fill:** $100–$150
- **Full flush with cleaning solution:** $150–$250
- **Luxury vehicles or specialty coolant:** $250–$400+

Dealer prices tend to run higher. Independent [radiator shops](/category/radiator-shop) and [auto repair shops](/category/auto-repair-shop) often charge less. Always get a written estimate before work begins. Our [FAQ](/faq) also covers general questions about radiator service pricing.

## Flush vs other cooling system repairs

- **Drain and fill** — Replaces coolant without deep cleaning
- **Radiator flush** — Cleans the system and replaces fluid
- **Radiator repair/replacement** — Fixes leaks or damaged radiators
- **Thermostat replacement** — Fixes stuck open/closed thermostats

If you also have overheating, low coolant, or white exhaust smoke, a flush may not be enough — you may need diagnosis first. [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair) can help you spot related problems. If the flush does not resolve the issue, the next step is usually deciding between [repair and replacement](/blogs/radiator-repair-vs-replacement-which-is-better).

## Tips before you book

1. **Use the coolant type specified** in your owner manual — mixing types can cause damage.
2. **Fix leaks first** — flushing a system with a bad hose or radiator wastes money.
3. **Compare quotes** from at least two local shops.
4. **Ask what is included** — labor, coolant, disposal fees, and a leak check should be clear.
