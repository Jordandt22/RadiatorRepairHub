A small radiator leak rarely stays small for long. Catching the problem early can save you from an overheated engine, a tow bill, and a much more expensive repair. If you are already losing coolant, read [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak) before deciding whether to keep driving.

## Warning signs to watch for

- **Puddles under the front of your car** — Coolant is often green, orange, pink, or blue. A sticky, sweet smell usually means antifreeze.
- **Rising temperature gauge** — If the needle climbs in normal traffic, your cooling system may be losing pressure.
- **Low coolant level** — Needing to top off coolant frequently is a red flag, even if you do not see an obvious leak yet.
- **White exhaust smoke** — Can point to a head gasket issue, but it is worth ruling out a cooling system problem first.
- **Heater stops working well** — Low coolant can reduce heat inside the cabin, especially in colder weather.

## Common causes

Radiator leaks often come from:

1. Corrosion in an older radiator or hose connection
2. A loose or worn hose clamp
3. A damaged radiator cap that no longer holds pressure
4. Road debris or age-related cracks in the radiator tank

## What to do if you suspect a leak

1. **Stop driving if the gauge is in the red.** Pull over safely and let the engine cool.
2. **Check the coolant level only when the engine is cold.** Opening a hot radiator cap is dangerous.
3. **Look for wet spots** around the radiator, hoses, and water pump.
