A small radiator leak rarely stays small for long. Catching the problem early can save you from an overheated engine, a tow bill, and a much more expensive repair. If you are already losing coolant, read [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak) before deciding whether to keep driving.

## Warning signs to watch for

- **Puddles under the front of your car** — Coolant is often green, orange, pink, or blue. A sticky, sweet smell usually means antifreeze. Compare other under-car drip causes in [Coolant Leak Under Car: Causes and What to Do](/blogs/coolant-leak-under-car-causes-and-what-to-do).
- **Rising temperature gauge** — If the needle climbs in normal traffic, your cooling system may be losing fluid or pressure.
- **Low coolant level** — Needing to top off coolant frequently is a red flag, even if you do not see an obvious puddle yet.
- **Steam or sweet smell from under the hood** — Often coolant hitting hot parts or boiling from low pressure / low level.
- **Heater stops working well** — Low coolant can reduce heat inside the cabin, especially in colder weather.
- **White exhaust smoke that smells sweet** — More likely an **internal** problem such as a head gasket than an external radiator drip. See [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak).

For a wider checklist, see [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair).

## Common causes

Coolant loss often comes from:

1. Corrosion or cracks in an older radiator core or plastic end tank
2. A loose, cracked, or worn hose and clamp
3. A damaged [radiator cap](/blogs/radiator-cap-symptoms-and-replacement) that no longer holds pressure
4. Water pump weeping or seal failure
5. Road debris impact or age-related tank cracks

A shop pressure test is the most reliable way to find small seeps that only show up once the system is pressurized.

## Cap vs hose vs radiator vs pump

Use these clues (engine cold) to describe symptoms to a technician:

| Likely area | What you might see |
|-------------|--------------------|
| **Cap / reservoir** | Overflow after driving; wetness around the filler neck; system fails a pressure test with no hose wetness |
| **Hose / clamp** | Wetness along upper/lower radiator hoses; soft or cracked rubber |
| **Radiator** | Wet core or tank seams; coolant on the radiator face; corrosion stains |
| **Water pump** | Drip near the weep hole or pump housing; sometimes a grinding belt-driven pump |

## What to do if you suspect a leak

1. **Stop driving if the gauge is in the red or you see steam.** Pull over safely and let the engine cool — see [Why Is My Car Overheating?](/blogs/why-is-my-car-overheating).
2. **Check the coolant level only when the engine is cold.** Opening a hot radiator cap is dangerous.
3. **Look for wet spots** around the radiator, hoses, water pump, and cap/reservoir.
4. **Skip DIY “stop leak” as a first step** unless a technician recommends it for a specific limp-home case — read [Is Radiator Stop Leak Safe?](/blogs/is-radiator-stop-leak-safe).
5. **Visit a cooling specialist** for a pressure test and quote. Browse [radiator repair services](/category/radiator-repair-service) or [radiator shops](/category/radiator-shop) near you.

Ignoring a leak can cascade into engine damage — see [Can a Bad Radiator Damage Your Engine?](/blogs/can-a-bad-radiator-damage-your-engine).
