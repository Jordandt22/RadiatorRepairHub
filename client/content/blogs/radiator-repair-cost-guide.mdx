Radiator repair costs vary widely depending on what is wrong, your vehicle, and where you live. This guide breaks down typical price ranges so you know what to expect before you call a shop. For context on what might need fixing, see [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair) or [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak).

## Common radiator repair costs

These are general U.S. estimates — always get a written quote for your specific vehicle.

- **Hose replacement:** $150–$350 (parts and labor)
- **Radiator cap replacement:** $15–$50 for the part; minimal labor if done during another service — see [Radiator Cap Symptoms & Replacement](/blogs/radiator-cap-symptoms-and-replacement)
- **Radiator leak repair (solder/seal):** $100–$300 when repairable — mostly older metal cores; cracked plastic end tanks usually require [full replacement](/blogs/radiator-repair-vs-replacement-which-is-better)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 — see [Thermostat vs Radiator](/blogs/thermostat-vs-radiator-whats-causing-overheating) if overheating is the main symptom
- **Electric cooling fan (fuse/relay):** Often $20–$150 when diagnosis confirms a simple electrical part
- **Electric fan motor / assembly:** Roughly $250–$600+ parts and labor depending on vehicle and dual-fan setups — see [Radiator Fan Not Working](/blogs/radiator-fan-not-working)
- **Water pump replacement:** $300–$750+
- **Radiator replacement:** $400–$900+ (economy vehicles often lower; trucks and luxury models higher)
- **Coolant flush:** Typically $100–$250 for most passenger cars; drain-and-fill can be lower, and luxury or specialty coolant jobs can run $250–$400+ — see our [Radiator Flush guide](/blogs/radiator-flush-what-to-expect-and-cost) for details

For typical job durations, see [How Long Does Radiator Repair Take?](/blogs/how-long-does-radiator-repair-take). When you are ready to compare shops, read [How to Choose a Radiator Shop](/blogs/how-to-choose-a-radiator-shop).

## What affects the price

1. **Vehicle make and model** — Parts availability and labor time differ significantly between a compact sedan and a heavy-duty truck.
2. **Type of repair** — A hose is far cheaper than a full radiator or head gasket work. Compare [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak) if diagnosis is unclear.
3. **Labor rates** — Urban areas and dealerships typically charge more than independent [radiator shops](/category/radiator-shop) or [auto repair shops](/category/auto-repair-shop).
4. **Parts quality** — OEM vs aftermarket radiators and hoses can change the total by hundreds of dollars.
5. **Additional damage** — Overheating from an ignored leak can lead to warped heads or blown gaskets — a much larger bill. Do not delay if your car is running hot; read [Why Is My Car Overheating?](/blogs/why-is-my-car-overheating).

## Repair vs replacement

- **Small external leak on a hose or clamp** — Repair or replace the hose
- **Corroded or cracked radiator tank** — Usually full radiator replacement
- **Clogged radiator with no leak** — Flush first; replace if flow does not improve
- **Repeated overheating after repairs** — Full system diagnosis

A [radiator repair specialist](/category/radiator-repair-service) can pressure-test the system and recommend the most cost-effective fix. For a detailed breakdown of each scenario, see [Radiator Repair vs Replacement: Which Is Better?](/blogs/radiator-repair-vs-replacement-which-is-better). If you are not sure how long your current radiator has left, see [How Long Does a Radiator Last?](/blogs/how-long-does-a-radiator-last).

## How to get an accurate quote

1. **Describe symptoms clearly** — leaks, overheating, steam, low coolant, warning lights.
2. **Ask what is included** — diagnosis fee, parts, labor, coolant refill, tax.
3. **Get it in writing** before authorizing major work.
4. **Compare 2–3 local shops** using [RadiatorRepairHub search](/search) to find options with reviews and contact info.

Our [FAQ](/faq) also answers common questions about pricing and repair times.

## Ways to avoid surprise costs

- Fix small leaks before they cause overheating
- Follow coolant flush intervals recommended by your manufacturer
- Do not ignore a rising temperature gauge — see [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak)
- Avoid temporary fixes like stop leak without understanding the tradeoffs — read [Is Radiator Stop Leak Safe?](/blogs/is-radiator-stop-leak-safe)
