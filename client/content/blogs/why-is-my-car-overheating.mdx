An overheating engine is one of the most serious problems you should not ignore on the road. The temperature gauge climbing into the red — or steam from under the hood — means your cooling system is failing to remove heat fast enough. Acting quickly protects your engine from costly damage.

## What to do immediately

1. **Pull over safely** and turn off the engine.
2. **Do not open the radiator cap** while the engine is hot — pressurized coolant can spray and cause burns.
3. **Wait until the engine is fully cool** before checking anything — often 30 minutes or longer after a severe overheat. Never open a pressurized cap while the system is hot.
4. **Call for a tow** if the gauge stays in the red, you see steam, or you are far from help.

Wondering if you can drive a short distance? Read [Can You Drive With a Radiator Leak?](/blogs/can-you-drive-with-a-radiator-leak) — the same caution applies whenever the engine is running hot.

## Common causes of overheating

### Low coolant or a leak

Coolant carries heat away from the engine. A leak in the radiator, a hose, or the water pump means less fluid and less cooling. Look for puddles, sweet smells, or a dropping reservoir level. See [How to Spot a Radiator Leak](/blogs/how-to-spot-a-radiator-leak) for warning signs, or [Coolant Leak Under Car: Causes and What to Do](/blogs/coolant-leak-under-car-causes-and-what-to-do) for help identifying the source.

### Failed thermostat

The thermostat regulates coolant flow. If it sticks closed, coolant cannot circulate and the engine overheats quickly — often within minutes of driving. Unsure whether it is the thermostat or the radiator? Read [Thermostat vs Radiator: What's Causing Overheating?](/blogs/thermostat-vs-radiator-whats-causing-overheating).

### Clogged or damaged radiator

Rust, debris, or internal blockages reduce the radiator's ability to dissipate heat. External damage from road debris can also cause leaks and overheating.

### Broken water pump

The water pump keeps coolant moving. A worn impeller or leaking pump seal can stop circulation and trigger rapid overheating.

### Head gasket failure

Coolant can leak internally into the engine, causing overheating without an obvious puddle. White exhaust smoke or milky oil are red flags. Learn more in [Radiator Leak vs Head Gasket Leak](/blogs/radiator-leak-vs-head-gasket-leak).

### Cooling fan not working

Electric fans should kick on at idle or in traffic. A bad fan motor, relay, or sensor can cause overheating in stop-and-go driving even when highway speeds feel fine. See [Radiator Fan Not Working: Symptoms and Repair](/blogs/radiator-fan-not-working) for diagnosis and repair details.

### Old or contaminated coolant

Neglected fluid loses its anti-corrosion properties and can clog the system. A [radiator flush](/blogs/radiator-flush-what-to-expect-and-cost) may help as maintenance — but not if you have an active leak or failed part.

## Signs you should not ignore

Review [7 Signs Your Radiator Needs Repair](/blogs/7-signs-your-radiator-needs-repair) for a full checklist. The biggest warning signs include:

- Temperature gauge in the red zone
- Steam from under the hood
- Coolant loss with no obvious explanation
- Heater blowing cold air at idle
- Repeated overheating after adding coolant

## How a shop diagnoses overheating

A technician will typically:

1. Pressure-test the cooling system for external leaks
2. Check thermostat operation and fan function
3. Inspect the water pump, hoses, and radiator
4. Test for head gasket failure if internal leaks are suspected

Start with a [radiator repair service](/category/radiator-repair-service) or [radiator shop](/category/radiator-shop) that handles cooling systems regularly. General [auto repair shops](/category/auto-repair-shop) can help too if they have the right equipment.

## Prevention tips

- Check coolant level monthly when the engine is cold
- Follow your manufacturer's flush and service intervals
- Fix small leaks before they become overheating events
- Watch the temperature gauge in summer traffic and when towing
